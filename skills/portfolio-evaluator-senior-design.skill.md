## PURPOSE & CONTEXT

This skill evaluates design case studies **as a Senior Product Designer would be assessed by hiring managers and design leaders**. It goes beyond surface-level feedback to identify what signals professional maturity and makes someone stand out for roles in complex product environments.

**Target audience:** Senior Product Designers (8-15 years) targeting:
- B2B SaaS (financial planning, compliance, data visualization)
- Fintech (payments, trading, risk management)
- Data-heavy products (analytics, forecasting, dashboards)
- Distributed/remote first companies

**Design context:** You run a design agency and conduct active job searches. This evaluates *both* case studies for personal branding and portfolio case studies for employment applications.

## EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

### Four Dimensions (Analytic Rubric)

Each dimension scored 1-5:
- **5 Exceptional** — Award-worthy, demonstrates mastery
- **4 Strong** — Professional, job-interview ready
- **3 Developing** — Clear intent, needs refinement
- **2 Weak** — Surface-level, lacks specificity
- **1 Poor** — Missing or unclear

---

## DIMENSION 1: ARCHITECTURE
*How well the case study is structured, scoped, and organized for comprehension*

### What We're Evaluating:
- **Clarity of scope** — Is it immediately clear what problem was solved?
- **Role specificity** — Does the writer clearly state what *they* did vs. team?
- **Context sufficiency** — Do we understand the business context and constraints?
- **Logical narrative flow** — Does the story progress naturally from problem → process → outcome?

### Scoring Guidance:

**5 Exceptional**
- Crystal-clear problem statement that frames the entire case study
- Explicit ownership stated early (e.g., "As sole designer, I..." or "Led design for...")
- Business context is rich: market, competitive landscape, or internal constraints are stated
- Sections flow logically; a reviewer can skim section headers and understand the arc
- Scope is appropriately bounded (not trying to cover too much; shows judgment)
- Handles ambiguity transparently (e.g., "We didn't have user research, so I...")

**4 Strong**
- Problem is stated clearly, though may take a sentence or two to crystallize
- Role is explained, but may not be in the opening
- Sufficient business context (enough to understand stakes, though not exhaustive)
- Good section organization; easy to follow the flow
- Scope is reasonable, though might be slightly ambitious
- Acknowledges constraints where relevant

**3 Developing**
- Problem is described, but requires inference from the context
- Role is mentioned but doesn't stand out; reader has to piece it together
- Minimal business context; reader must infer what matters
- Organization exists but may feel disjointed; some backtracking
- Scope feels loose or overly ambitious for the space
- Constraints are mentioned in passing, not integrated into the narrative

**2 Weak**
- Problem is buried or vague ("redesign the dashboard")
- Role is ambiguous; unclear what the writer personally did
- Little to no business context; feels disconnected from real stakes
- Organization is chaotic; hard to follow the sequence of events
- Tries to cover too much ground; reader feels lost
- Doesn't acknowledge constraints or challenges

**1 Poor**
- No clear problem statement
- Role is not mentioned or is generic ("worked on design")
- No business context whatsoever
- Unorganized; lacks structure
- Scope is all over the place
- No attempt to explain the landscape or situation

## DIMENSION 2: NARRATIVE
*How compelling, authentic, and strategically resonant the story is*

### What We're Evaluating:
- **Emotional resonance** — Does the reader *care* about solving this problem?
- **Authenticity** — Does it sound like a real human reflecting, or templated/generic?
- **Decision transparency** — Are trade-offs, failures, and pivots visible?
- **Relevance to target role** — Does it signal the candidate understands the job they're interviewing for?
- **Reflection & learning** — Evidence of evolution, not just execution

### Scoring Guidance:

**5 Exceptional**
- Opens with a concrete problem that hooks the reader (specific metric, user pain, or business challenge)
- Reader feels invested in the outcome—not because of hype, but because the stakes are clear
- Authentic voice throughout; sounds like a person, not a template
- Decisional moments are visible: "We considered X, Y, and Z. We chose X because..."
- Acknowledges what didn't work or what was learned: "This taught me..."
- Explicitly connects the project to the target role/company (e.g., if applying to fintech, shows understanding of regulatory/trust challenges)
- Shows evidence of *thinking*, not just *doing*

**4 Strong**
- Problem is compelling, though may not hook immediately
- Engaging voice; feels authentic most of the time
- Some decision-making is visible; reader understands key choices
- Learning is mentioned, though may be brief
- Shows some awareness of job relevance (e.g., mentions users, business impact)
- Clear evidence of intentional design thinking

**3 Developing**
- Problem is stated but doesn't naturally draw the reader in
- Voice is somewhat generic or templated
- Decisions are mentioned but not *why* (e.g., "We A/B tested" without explaining the hypothesis)
- Learning exists but feels obligatory ("This taught me to listen to users")
- Limited connection to target role; reads as generic case study
- Design thinking is present but not deeply examined

**2 Weak**
- Problem is stated flatly ("We needed to improve conversion")
- Voice is templated or corporate; doesn't sound like a person
- Decisions are listed as activities, not reasoning ("We did research, then design, then testing")
- Learning is absent or clichéd
- No attempt to connect to the job being pursued
- Reads as a checklist of activities, not a strategic narrative

**1 Poor**
- Problem is vague or missing
- No authentic voice; entirely templated
- No visibility into decision-making
- No evidence of learning or reflection
- No attempt to signal understanding of the role or company
- Reads as padding around a portfolio project

## DIMENSION 3: VISUAL DESIGN & CLARITY
*How effectively visuals, typography, and layout support comprehension and engagement*

### What We're Evaluating:
- **Visual hierarchy** — Are the most important ideas emphasized?
- **Scannability** — Can a busy hiring manager skim this in 2-3 minutes and get the gist?
- **Image quality & context** — Are screenshots, flows, or wireframes clear? Do captions explain what we're seeing?
- **Consistency** — Do layouts, fonts, and colors feel cohesive, or chaotic?
- **Whitespace & pacing** — Is there breathing room, or is it dense?
- **Relevance of visuals** — Do the images serve the narrative, or are they decorative?

### Scoring Guidance:

**5 Exceptional**
- Strong visual hierarchy: headings, key stats, or critical images stand out
- Can be scanned in 2-3 minutes; a skimmer gets the story
- Images are high-quality, well-composed, and labeled with context captions
- Consistent use of typography, color, and spacing throughout
- Whitespace is generous; breathing room between sections
- Every visual has a purpose and advances the narrative
- Visual language reflects the brand/product being designed (not generic)
- Design doesn't distract from content; it enables clarity

**4 Strong**
- Good hierarchy; important ideas are visually emphasized
- Scannable in 3-5 minutes
- Images are clear and mostly have context captions
- Consistent design language, though some minor inconsistencies
- Adequate whitespace; sections are visually separated
- Most visuals serve the narrative
- No significant distractions

**3 Developing**
- Hierarchy exists but is subtle; important ideas aren't always emphasized
- Requires 5+ minutes to scan; some hunting for key information
- Images are clear but not all have context captions
- Design language is somewhat consistent; some tonal shifts
- Whitespace is adequate but could be more generous
- Some images feel decorative or tangential
- Design supports content, though not always elegantly

**2 Weak**
- Weak hierarchy; hard to tell what's important
- Dense layout; requires sustained reading
- Images lack captions or are unclear (reader must guess what they're seeing)
- Inconsistent design language; feels disjointed
- Limited whitespace; feels cramped
- Many visuals feel tangential or decorative
- Design choices are distracting or confusing

**1 Poor**
- No clear hierarchy
- Very dense; impossible to skim
- Images are unclear or missing context
- Chaotic design; no consistent visual language
- No whitespace; overwhelming
- Visuals don't support the narrative
- Design actively hinders comprehension

## DIMENSION 4: IMPACT & OUTCOMES
*How effectively the case study connects design work to business or user outcomes*

### What We're Evaluating:
- **Measurable outcomes** — Are there metrics (adoption, engagement, conversion, time savings, revenue)?
- **Outcome ambition** — Was the target meaningful, not incremental?
- **Causal clarity** — Does the case study make a credible link between design and outcome?
- **User value articulation** — Is it clear how users benefited, not just the business?
- **Honest framing** — If impact is modest or indirect, does the writer own it?

### Scoring Guidance:

**5 Exceptional**
- **Primary outcome:** Specific, ambitious metric with before/after (e.g., "Reduced onboarding time from 45 min to 12 min")
- **Secondary outcome:** Often includes user satisfaction, adoption, or business metric
- Causal chain is clear: "This design choice led to this behavior, which led to this metric"
- User value is explicit (e.g., "Users saved 30 min/week" or "Reduced error rate by X%, improving reliability")
- If impact was indirect or modest, writer owns it: "We couldn't measure direct revenue impact, but we influenced the roadmap for Q3"
- Metric is business-relevant to the target company (e.g., if applying to fintech, mentions trust/compliance outcomes)
- Quantified impact makes the outcome memorable

**4 Strong**
- Clear, specific outcome with a metric (e.g., "Increased engagement by 23%")
- Outcome is business-relevant and meaningful
- Causal link is explained, though may be slightly simplified
- User value is mentioned
- Writer acknowledges any limitations in measurement
- Outcome is credible and proportional to the scope of work

**3 Developing**
- Outcome is stated but may be somewhat vague (e.g., "Improved user satisfaction")
- Metric may be qualitative ("users said the new flow was clearer") or semi-quantified
- Causal link is implied but not explicitly drawn
- User value is touched upon
- Limited acknowledgment of measurement limitations
- Outcome is credible but modest

**2 Weak**
- Outcome is stated generally ("The redesign was successful")
- Metric is missing or unmeasured
- No causal link between design and outcome
- User value is absent
- No acknowledgment of how outcome was measured
- Outcome feels disconnected from the work shown

**1 Poor**
- No outcome stated
- No metric or measurement
- No causal link
- No user value articulated
- No evidence of impact
- Reads as "I did design work" with no connection to what it accomplished

## COMPOSITE SCORING & INTERPRETATION

### Scoring Formula:
Sum all four dimension scores (max 20 points).

**Translate to letter grade:**
- **18-20 (A+)** — Exceptional portfolio; strong signals for senior/lead interviews
- **16-17 (A)** — Strong portfolio; job-interview ready; good competitive position
- **14-15 (B+)** — Solid portfolio; needs 1-2 refinements to stand out
- **12-13 (B)** — Developing portfolio; clear opportunities for improvement
- **10-11 (C)** — Weak portfolio; significant work needed before submitting
- **<10 (C-)** — Not ready; restart with a different project or major rebuild

### What Each Grade Signals:

**A+ (18-20)** — This case study signals senior/principal-level thinking. The writer demonstrates not just craft but *judgment*: they scope appropriately, navigate ambiguity, reflect critically, and connect design to business outcomes. This would advance you in later-round interviews against peers.

**A (16-17)** — This case study is compelling and well-executed. It's definitely job-interview material and signals a strong designer. In a competitive search, you'd want most of your portfolio at this level or higher.

**B+ (14-15)** — Good case study with clear strengths. Identify 1-2 weak spots and revise. This is borderline interview-ready; the refinements could move it to A territory.

**B (12-13)** — Case study has merit but needs work. Identify which dimension is weakest and prioritize that. Return to this project once you've revised.

**C (10-11)** — Not yet ready for your target audience. Either revise significantly or set this project aside and feature a stronger one.

## HOW TO EVALUATE A CASE STUDY

### Step 1: Gather Context
Before scoring, collect:
- The case study itself (URL, PDF, or image file)
- The target role/company (if available)
- Any background on the project (team size, timeline, constraints)

### Step 2: Read Twice
**First read (5 min):** Skim. Get the story. What sticks with you?

**Second read (10 min):** Slow down. For each dimension, mentally note:
- What's exemplary?
- What's missing or weak?
- What could be improved with one targeted revision?

### Step 3: Score Each Dimension
For each of the four dimensions (Architecture, Narrative, Visual Design, Impact):
- Assign a 1-5 score based on the guidance above
- Note 1-2 specific observations (e.g., "Role clarity is strong—stated in opening. But business context is minimal.")

### Step 4: Composite Score & Interpretation
Add up the four dimension scores. Interpret the result using the letter-grade scale above.

### Step 5: Prioritized Feedback
Identify **one primary opportunity** for improvement.
- Not all dimensions need to be 5s (that's rare).
- If you scored 4, 4, 3, 3 = B+ (14), the third and fourth dimensions are your leverage. Strengthening either of those to a 4 would lift you to A territory (16).

## EXAMPLES: HOW TO APPLY THE FRAMEWORK

### Example 1: Kushki Product Redesign (Your Leadership Case Study)

**Project:** Design system rebuild + team scaling at Kushki (fintech)

**Dimension Scores:**

**Architecture: 5**
- Opens with clear problem: "Kushki was scaling, but our design was brittle. I inherited three designers and a system that couldn't scale."
- Role is crystal-clear: "I led the system rebuild and mentored two junior designers through it."
- Business context is rich: Payment processing, regulatory environment, developer handoffs.
- Flow: Challenge → People strategy → System redesign → Outcomes.
- Shows judgment: "We didn't rebuild everything. We focused on highest-impact patterns first."

**Narrative: 5**
- Hooks with a real leadership challenge: "Scaling design isn't about pixels; it's about judgment under ambiguity."
- Authentic voice throughout; you reflect on what mentoring taught you.
- Visible decision-making: "We chose to invest in tokens early because [reason]."
- Growth arc: "I learned that I'm better at coaching through constraints than I am at building systems from scratch."
- Connects to target (e.g., if applying to Thoughtworks): Shows your leadership philosophy aligns with coaching, evidence-driven decisions, and strategic thinking.

**Visual Design: 4**
- Strong hierarchy; key metrics stand out.
- Scannable in 3-4 minutes.
- Token system and component examples are clear.
- One thing: The before/after comparison could use clearer labels (which one is the old system?).
- Overall polish is high; design language is cohesive.

**Impact: 5**
- Measurable: "Reduced design-to-handoff time by 40%; design-to-dev bug rate dropped from 12% to 3%."
- User value: "Developers ship faster and with fewer rework cycles."
- Business value: "Onboarding a new product line went from 3 months to 6 weeks design work."
- Causal clarity: Token-first approach → consistency → faster handoffs → time savings.
- Honest framing: "Revenue impact was indirect, but product teams reported faster iteration."

**Total: 19/20 (A+)**

### Example 2: Fincast Redesign (Solo Designer, Smaller Scope)

**Project:** Product pivot at Fincast; redesigned flow for financial planning tool

**Dimension Scores:**

**Architecture: 3**
- Problem is clear: "Product team wanted to shift from automated forecasting to scenario planning, requiring a UX rethink."
- Role is stated: "As sole designer..."
- But: Business context is thin. Why was the pivot necessary? What was the market pressure or internal constraint?
- Flow is logical but feels a bit linear: problem → user research → design → outcomes. No mention of constraints or trade-offs.

**Narrative: 4**
- Engaging opening: "Our forecasting was accurate, but users wanted to *explore*."
- Authentic voice; sounds like genuine reflection.
- Decision-making is shown: "We ran a workshop with product; several ideas emerged, but I recommended scenario tables first because..."
- Learning is brief: "This taught me to front-load conversations with dev about interaction feasibility."
- Relevance: For a B2B SaaS role, this signals good product partnership. Could be stronger if you explicitly named a company or role you're targeting.

**Visual Design: 4**
- Good hierarchy; key flows stand out.
- Scannable in 3-4 minutes.
- Before/after comparison is clear.
- Captions explain the design intent nicely.
- Minor: One wireframe page has dense notes; could break into two.

**Impact: 3**
- Outcome is stated: "Adoption of scenario planning jumped from 12% to 34% of DAUs."
- But: Causal link is fuzzy. Did adoption increase because we redesigned the interaction, or because product marketed it differently?
- User value is implied ("Users can now explore") but not deeply explored. Did they save time? Did they make better decisions?
- No mention of how outcome was measured (qualitative feedback, instrumentation, user interviews?).

**Total: 14/20 (B+)**

**Feedback:** This is interview-ready but not yet standout. Opportunity: Strengthen the **Impact** dimension by adding one qualitative insight (e.g., quote from a user or PM about the value of scenario exploration) and clarifying the causal chain. Also: Add a sentence or two of **business context** to the Architecture section (Why did fintech scenario planning matter at that moment? Was there competitor pressure?).

## COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO AVOID THEM

| Pitfall | Why It Hurts | How to Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Role ambiguity** ("We redesigned the dashboard") | Hiring managers can't tell what *you* contributed vs. the team | Lead with your specific role: "As the sole designer, I..." or "I led the visual design while the PM owned strategy." |
| **Activity-focused, not outcome-focused** ("We conducted user research, created wireframes, ran A/B tests") | Reads as a checklist; no sense of *why* or *what it accomplished* | Reframe around decisions: "Research revealed users couldn't distinguish risk levels. I redesigned the visual hierarchy..." |
| **Business context is invisible** (starts with design solution) | Reader doesn't understand the stakes; feels disconnected | Add 2-3 sentences of context upfront: market, product stage, or internal constraint. |
| **Impact is unmeasured or vague** ("Users loved it"; "The redesign was successful") | Sounds nice but doesn't prove anything; senior hires expect rigor | Add metrics: adoption % change, engagement, error reduction, time savings, or revenue. Even "qualitative feedback from 5 users showed X" is better than nothing. |
| **Narrative is templated** (follows a rigid problem-solution-outcome template verbatim) | Sounds like it could be about any project; lacks authenticity | Inject your voice: Show trade-offs, failed experiments, pivots, or lessons learned. Let the reader hear *you* thinking. |
| **Visuals are generic or decorative** (stock imagery, unrelated screenshots) | Distracts or confuses; signals lack of craft | Every image should serve the story. Use real screenshots, annotated flows, or before/after comparisons. |
| **Scope is sprawling** (tries to cover too much ground) | Reader feels lost; hard to follow the narrative | Tighten the scope. A single feature redesign or a team-building initiative is stronger than "rebuilt the entire product." |

## TIPS FOR TARGETED CASE STUDY DEVELOPMENT

### If You're Targeting B2B SaaS (Financial Planning, Analytics):
- Emphasize your understanding of complex domains (compliance, data viz, integration)
- Show evidence of cross-functional thinking (how design balanced product roadmap vs. user needs)
- Highlight outcomes that matter to SaaS: adoption, retention, NPS, or revenue impact
- If you've worked with remote or distributed teams, signal that

### If You're Targeting Fintech:
- Explicitly address trust, security, or regulatory constraints
- Show how design *simplified* a complex problem
- Emphasize measurable outcomes (time savings, error reduction, compliance)
- If you've designed under constraint (limited API, legacy systems), highlight your problem-solving

### If You're Targeting Data-Heavy Products:
- Show your understanding of data viz principles, interaction patterns, or information hierarchy
- Demonstrate how you made complex data accessible
- Emphasize outcomes: Did people understand the data better? Did they make better decisions faster?
- If you've worked on dashboards, reporting tools, or analytics, lead with that expertise

### For Remote-First / Async-Friendly Companies:
- Highlight how you communicated design in writing (case studies, design briefs, decision docs)
- Show evidence of asynchronous feedback and iteration
- Emphasize clarity and documentation (design systems, handoffs)

## SELF-EVALUATION WORKFLOW

1. **Select a case study** from your portfolio
2. **Read it cold** — as if you're a busy hiring manager with 2 minutes
3. **Score using the rubric** above
4. **Identify the weakest dimension**
5. **Revise that dimension** (don't try to improve all four at once)
6. **Re-score** and compare
7. **Repeat** until you're at A territory (16+)

## ADAPTATION: USING THIS FOR PEER REVIEW

If you're reviewing a peer's work:
1. Share this skill/rubric alongside your feedback
2. Score independently
3. Compare scores with the peer
4. Focus on **gaps between your assessment and theirs** — that's where the insight lives
5. Frame feedback around the rubric: "Your Architecture scored 4/5 (strong). Your Impact scored 2/5 (weak). That gap suggests you have the structure right, but you're not connecting your work to outcomes clearly enough."

This approach is more actionable than "I think your case study is good but could be better."
